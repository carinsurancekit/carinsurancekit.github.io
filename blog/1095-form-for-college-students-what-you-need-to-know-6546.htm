1095 Form for College Students: What You Need to Know

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

Navigating the complexities of tax forms can be daunting, especially for college students who are often filing taxes for the first time. The 1095 Form is one such document that might seem confusing at first glance, but understanding it is crucial for staying compliant with U.S. healthcare laws. Whether you're covered under your parents' plan, a university-sponsored policy, or a marketplace plan, this guide will break down everything you need to know about the 1095 Form—and how it intersects with today’s most pressing issues, from healthcare accessibility to the rising cost of education.

Why the 1095 Form Matters for Students

The 1095 Form is part of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) reporting system, designed to verify that individuals have qualifying health coverage. For college students, this form serves as proof of insurance, which is especially important given the high costs of medical care and the risks of being uninsured.

Types of 1095 Forms

There are three versions of the 1095 Form, each serving a slightly different purpose:

  1. 1095-A – Issued to students who purchased insurance through the Health Insurance Marketplace.
  2. 1095-B – Sent by insurance providers (including university health plans) to confirm coverage.
  3. 1095-C – Provided by employers (relevant for students with part-time or full-time jobs offering benefits).

Most students will receive either a 1095-B (if covered under a university or private plan) or a 1095-A (if they enrolled via the Marketplace).

How the 1095 Form Affects Your Taxes

Filing taxes as a student can be tricky, especially when healthcare reporting is involved. Here’s what you need to know:

Do You Need to File a 1095 Form with Your Taxes?

Technically, no—you don’t submit the 1095 Form itself with your tax return. However, the information on it (such as months covered) is used to fill out Form 8962 if you received premium tax credits through the Marketplace.

Penalties for Lack of Coverage

While the federal individual mandate penalty was reduced to $0 in 2019, some states (like California and Massachusetts) still impose fines for being uninsured. If you didn’t have coverage, you might need to claim an exemption or pay a state penalty.

The Bigger Picture: Healthcare and College Affordability

The 1095 Form isn’t just a bureaucratic requirement—it’s tied to larger debates about healthcare access and student financial stability.

The Rising Cost of University Health Plans

Many colleges require students to enroll in their health plans, which can cost $2,000–$4,000 per year. For low-income students, this adds to the already crushing burden of tuition and living expenses. Some opt to waive university coverage if they have a comparable plan (like a parent’s insurance), but navigating waivers can be confusing.

Mental Health Coverage Gaps

With mental health crises among students at an all-time high, verifying that your insurance covers counseling and psychiatric care is critical. The 1095-B or 1095-C can help you confirm whether your plan meets ACA standards for mental health services.

Common Mistakes Students Make with the 1095 Form

Even the most diligent students can stumble when dealing with healthcare paperwork. Here are some pitfalls to avoid:

1. Ignoring the Form Altogether

Some students assume that if they’re claimed as dependents, they don’t need to worry about the 1095. Wrong. Even if you’re on your parents’ plan, you should keep a copy for your records.

2. Misunderstanding Deadlines

The IRS requires insurers to mail 1095 Forms by March 2, but delays happen. If you haven’t received yours by mid-March, contact your provider or university health office.

3. Overlooking State-Specific Rules

If you attend school in a different state from your permanent address, check both states’ healthcare laws. For example, New York requires all residents to have coverage, even if they’re only there for college.

How to Get Help with Your 1095 Form

Confused? You’re not alone. Here are some resources:

  • University Health Centers – Many schools offer tax and insurance workshops.
  • IRS Free File Program – For students earning less than $73,000 annually.
  • Healthcare.gov – Live chat and phone support for Marketplace-related questions.

The Future of Healthcare Reporting

With political debates raging over Medicare for All and the ACA’s future, the 1095 Form’s role may evolve. For now, staying informed ensures you avoid penalties and make the most of your coverage—whether you’re dealing with a surprise medical bill or just trying to file taxes stress-free.

So, next time that 1095 arrives in your mailbox, don’t stash it in a drawer. Take a few minutes to review it—your wallet (and peace of mind) will thank you.
