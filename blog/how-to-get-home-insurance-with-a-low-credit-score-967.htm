How to Get Home Insurance with a Low Credit Score

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

In today’s volatile economy, maintaining a high credit score isn’t always easy. Job losses, medical emergencies, or unexpected financial setbacks can quickly drag your credit down. Unfortunately, a low credit score doesn’t just affect your ability to secure loans—it can also make getting affordable home insurance a challenge.

Insurance companies often use credit-based insurance scores to assess risk. A lower score might label you as a higher-risk policyholder, leading to higher premiums or even denials. But don’t lose hope. Even with a less-than-perfect credit score, you can still find ways to secure home insurance without breaking the bank.

Why Credit Scores Matter in Home Insurance

How Insurers Use Your Credit Score

Most insurers rely on credit-based insurance scores (different from FICO scores) to predict the likelihood of you filing a claim. Studies suggest that individuals with lower credit scores tend to file more claims. While this isn’t always fair, it’s a reality many homeowners must navigate.

States That Restrict Credit-Based Insurance Scoring

Not all states allow insurers to use credit scores as a pricing factor. If you live in California, Massachusetts, or Hawaii, insurers cannot deny coverage or charge higher premiums based solely on your credit. However, in most other states, your credit will play a role in your home insurance rates.

Steps to Secure Home Insurance with Bad Credit

1. Shop Around for Insurers Who Are More Lenient

Not all insurance companies weigh credit scores equally. Some providers specialize in high-risk policies and may offer better rates despite poor credit. Independent insurance agents can help you compare quotes from multiple carriers.

2. Improve Your Credit Before Applying

If you have time, take steps to boost your credit before applying for home insurance:
- Pay down debt – Lowering your credit utilization can improve your score.
- Dispute errors – Check your credit report for inaccuracies and dispute them.
- Avoid new credit inquiries – Multiple hard inquiries can further damage your score.

3. Consider a Higher Deductible

Opting for a higher deductible can lower your premium. If you’re confident you won’t file small claims, this strategy can offset the impact of a low credit score.

4. Bundle Your Insurance Policies

Many insurers offer discounts if you bundle home and auto insurance. Even with bad credit, bundling could make your overall insurance costs more manageable.

5. Look for Non-Standard or FAIR Plans

If traditional insurers deny you, state-run Fair Access to Insurance Requirements (FAIR) plans provide coverage for high-risk homeowners. These plans are typically more expensive but ensure you’re not left uninsured.

6. Work with an Independent Agent

Independent agents have access to multiple insurers and can find companies more willing to overlook credit issues. They may also negotiate better terms on your behalf.

Alternative Strategies for High-Risk Homeowners

Government Assistance Programs

Some federal and state programs assist low-income or high-risk homeowners in securing affordable insurance. Research options like FEMA’s National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or state-specific assistance initiatives.

Improving Home Safety to Lower Premiums

Even with bad credit, making your home safer can reduce insurance costs:
- Install security systems – Burglar alarms and smoke detectors may qualify for discounts.
- Upgrade roofing and plumbing – Mitigating risks like leaks or storm damage can make you a more attractive policyholder.
- Join a neighborhood watch – Some insurers offer discounts for community safety efforts.

Paying Premiums Upfront or in Installments

If possible, paying your annual premium in full (rather than monthly) can sometimes reduce costs. Alternatively, setting up automatic payments may prevent lapses that further hurt your credit.

Long-Term Fixes: Rebuilding Credit for Better Rates

While the above strategies help in the short term, improving your credit will yield long-term benefits. Over time, a higher score can qualify you for better insurance rates, lower interest loans, and more financial flexibility.

Monitoring Your Credit Regularly

Use free credit monitoring services to track changes and detect fraud early. Staying proactive helps prevent further damage to your score.

Using Secured Credit Cards

Rebuilding credit often starts with secured credit cards. These require a deposit but report positive payment history to credit bureaus, gradually improving your score.

Avoiding New Debt

While it’s tempting to open new credit lines, doing so can lower your average account age and increase risk in insurers’ eyes. Focus on paying existing debts first.

Final Thoughts

A low credit score doesn’t have to lock you out of home insurance. By shopping strategically, improving your home’s safety, and taking steps to rebuild credit, you can find coverage that fits your budget. The key is persistence—don’t settle for the first high quote you receive. With the right approach, even homeowners with financial challenges can protect their most valuable asset.
