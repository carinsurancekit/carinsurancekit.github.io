What to Expect During a Commercial Property Adjuster Visit

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

The world feels more unpredictable than ever. From the increasing frequency and severity of climate-related weather events to global supply chain disruptions and the lingering economic aftershocks of recent years, protecting your business’s physical assets has never been more critical. When disaster strikes—be it a hurricane, a flood, a fire, or even a burst pipe—the process of filing an insurance claim can feel like a second storm to weather. At the center of this process is a key figure: the commercial property adjuster. Understanding what to expect during their visit is not just about procedure; it's about empowerment, preparation, and ensuring your business can recover and rebuild on solid footing.

The adjuster's role is multifaceted. They are investigators, analysts, and negotiators, all rolled into one. They work for the insurance company, but a professional and ethical adjuster seeks to understand the full scope of the loss to facilitate a fair settlement based on the terms of your policy. Walking into this process prepared can significantly impact the efficiency and outcome of your claim.

Before the Adjuster Arrives: Laying the Groundwork for Success

The clock starts ticking the moment you discover the damage, but your preparation begins long before the adjuster schedules their visit. This pre-visit phase is where you can gain the most control over the situation.

1. Immediate Post-Loss Actions: Safety and Mitigation

Your first priority is always the safety of people. Once that is secured, your next responsibility is to prevent further damage. This is a standard clause in most commercial property policies. If a hole in the roof is letting in rain, you must arrange for a tarp. If a broken window is exposing your inventory to the elements, you must board it up. Keep all receipts for these emergency repairs and materials; they are a legitimate part of your claim. Failure to mitigate can be grounds for reducing your settlement.

2. Documenting Everything: The Power of Visual Evidence

Before you move a single piece of debris or clean up any water, document the scene extensively. In the age of smartphones, there is no excuse for not having a comprehensive visual record. * Photographs and Videos: Take wide-angle shots to establish context and close-ups to show specific damage. Capture everything—from structural damage to walls and ceilings, to damaged inventory, equipment, and furniture. Don’t forget to photograph serial numbers on damaged electronics and machinery. * Create an Inventory: Start a detailed list of all damaged items. For each item, note the description, approximate age, original purchase price, and estimated replacement cost. This will be a monumental task later, so starting early is key. * Secure Business Records: Gather your financial documents, including profit and loss statements, tax returns, and payroll records. These may be crucial for proving a business interruption loss.

3. Reviewing Your Insurance Policy

You do not want to be reading your policy for the first time with the adjuster. Before they arrive, re-familiarize yourself with its key sections: * Coverage Limits: What is the maximum your policy will pay? * Deductibles: How much are you responsible for paying out-of-pocket? * Covered Perils: What specific events are covered (e.g., fire, wind, vandalism)? * Exclusions: What is explicitly not covered (e.g., flood, earthquake, which often require separate policies)? * Endorsements: Are there any special add-ons or riders to your policy?

The Day of the Inspection: A Collaborative, Not Confrontational, Meeting

Think of the adjuster's visit as a fact-finding mission, not an interrogation. Your goal is to be a cooperative and organized source of information.

1. Who Should Be Present?

It is crucial to have the right people in the room. At a minimum, this should include: * The Property Owner or a Senior Manager: Someone with authority to answer questions and make decisions. * Your Key Maintenance or Facilities Manager: The person who knows the building's systems inside and out. * Your Contractor or Restoration Lead: Having your own professional estimator present is one of the most powerful things you can do. They can speak the same technical language as the adjuster, point out damage you might miss, and provide a preliminary repair estimate. This creates a checks-and-balances dynamic from the start. * Your Public Adjuster or Attorney (if you have hired one): They will act as your advocate and handle the primary communication and negotiation.

2. The Walk-Through: A Guided Tour of the Damage

The physical inspection is the core of the visit. The adjuster will want to see every inch of the affected area. Be prepared to lead the tour. * Point Out All Damage: Do not assume the adjuster will see everything. Politely and systematically point out every area of concern, even seemingly minor ones. * Explain the Cause: Tell the adjuster what you believe caused the damage. "The wind lifted this section of the roof, which allowed water to pour in and damage the server room below." * Discuss Pre-Loss Condition: Be honest about the condition of your property before the loss. If the roof was 20 years old, say so. Misrepresenting the pre-loss condition can damage your credibility. The adjuster will be assessing for pre-existing wear and tear, which is not covered. * Show Your Documentation: Provide the adjuster with copies (never originals) of the photos, videos, and inventory lists you created. This demonstrates your organization and reinforces your claim.

3. The Interview: Questions to Anticipate

The adjuster will have many questions. Answer them honestly and concisely. * The Timeline: "When did you first discover the damage?" "What was the last time the property was completely normal?" * The Cause: "What do you think happened?" "Have you had any similar issues in the past?" * Mitigation Efforts: "What have you done to secure the property?" "Can you provide receipts for the emergency services?" * Business Impact: "How has this event affected your operations?" "Have you had to temporarily close or relocate?" This line of questioning is critical for a business interruption claim.

Navigating Modern Complexities in the Claims Process

Today's commercial property claims are not just about repairing a wall. They are intertwined with contemporary global challenges.

1. Supply Chain Issues and Inflation

The post-pandemic world is defined by supply chain volatility and soaring material costs. A repair estimate based on pre-2020 pricing is obsolete. This is a critical point of discussion with the adjuster. * Discuss Replacement Cost Value (RCV) vs. Actual Cash Value (ACV): Most policies pay RCV, which is the cost to replace the damaged property with new materials of like kind and quality, without a deduction for depreciation. However, the final settlement must reflect today's reality of lumber, steel, and labor costs. Your contractor's estimate will be vital here. * Be Prepared for Delays: The adjuster may note that certain materials or specialized equipment are on backorder. This can affect the timeline and potentially the cost of repairs, which should be factored into additional living expenses or business interruption calculations.

2. Climate Change and Catastrophic Events

In an era of "mega-fires" and "500-year floods" happening with alarming regularity, insurance companies are increasingly scrutinizing the root cause of damage. * Concurrent Causation: This is a complex but crucial concept. What if a hurricane (covered) causes a storm surge (flood, often not covered) that damages your property? The adjuster will be meticulously determining the "efficient proximate cause" of the loss. Your policy language is paramount here. * Code Upgrades: After a widespread disaster, local building codes are often updated. Your old building may need to be rebuilt to new, more stringent codes (e.g., higher wind resistance, flood-proofing). Many policies include "Ordinance or Law" coverage for this, but you need to confirm its limits. The adjuster will assess what upgrades are legally required.

3. Cybersecurity and Physical Damage

The line between digital and physical worlds is blurring. A cyberattack that shuts down a building's management system could lead to a pipe freezing and bursting. The adjuster will need to investigate and establish the direct link between a covered peril (like water damage) and an excluded one (like a cyber event) to determine coverage.

After the Adjuster Leaves: The Path to Settlement

The visit is over, but the process is not. The adjuster will now compile their findings, review your policy, and create a detailed estimate using industry-standard software like Xactimate.

  • The Adjuster's Report: This report will include the scope of repairs and the estimated cost. It will be submitted to the insurance company for approval.
  • The Proof of Loss: You may be required to sign a formal "Proof of Loss" statement, which is a sworn statement of the amount you are claiming.
  • Reviewing the Settlement Offer: When you receive the initial settlement offer, review it meticulously with your contractor and/or public adjuster. Compare it line-by-line with your own estimates.
  • Negotiation is Normal: It is standard practice to negotiate. If you disagree with the scope or pricing in the adjuster's estimate, provide a detailed, written rebuttal with supporting evidence (e.g., your contractor's quote, photos of missed damage).

The relationship with your commercial property adjuster is a professional one, built on documentation, transparency, and a mutual understanding of a complex process. By being thoroughly prepared, understanding the modern context of claims, and knowing what to expect during their visit, you transform from a passive claimant into an active participant in your business's recovery. This proactive approach is your best strategy for navigating the aftermath of a disaster and securing the resources needed to rebuild, reopen, and move forward with confidence.
